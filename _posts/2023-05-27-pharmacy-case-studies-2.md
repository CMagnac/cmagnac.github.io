* Quasym&reg; (MPH 30% IR/ 70% SR) LP 20mg: 4 capsules per day at once
* Ritaline&reg; (MPH 0% IR/100% SR) 10mg: 2 tablets per day

The similarities between Ritaline&reg; and Quasym&reg; are:

* Their starting dose, which is 20mg.
* Their titration, which is the multiple of 2 starting dose every 3 to 7 days.
* Their maximal dose which is 60mg per days based on the "Autorisation de Mise sur le Marché" (AMM, the French Regulatory Affairs) and 1.2mg/kg per day for certain recommandations.

The main **difference** between Ritaline&reg; and Quasym&reg; is :

* Their pharmacokinetics properties, such as the concentration of drug peak. Ritaline&reg; has a concentration drug peak of 20 to 60 minutes and an efficacy of 6 to 8 hours, while Quasym&reg; has a concentration drug peak of 1 to 2 hours and an efficacy of 8 to 10 hours.

Indeed, the pharmacokinetics properties are related to the galenic form, which is 30% IR and 70% SR for Quasym&reg; (MPH 30% IR/ 70% SR) and 0% IR and 100% SR for Ritaline&reg;.

* Quetiapine SR 300mg: 1 tablet at night

Patients with ADHD can improve their **lifestyle** by following these pieces of advice:

* Accept their condition.
* Have healthy food in their pantry.
* Engage in physical activity for at least 30 minutes every day.
* Improve their mental health by avoiding screen time at least 2 hours before bedtime.
* Maintain consistency in all aspects of their lives (work, friends, family, etc.).

Let's focus on the main side effects of Methylphenidate.

**Mild side effects** include agitation, an energized feeling, headaches, difficulty sleeping, moodiness, anxiety, nausea, and reduced appetite.

Serious side effects may include:

* Cardiovascular reactions, such as sudden death, stroke, and heart attack.
* Increased blood pressure and heart rate (tachycardia).
* Psychiatric adverse reactions, including worsening of pre-existing psychiatric conditions.
* Development of new psychotic or manic symptoms.
* Sustained and sometimes painful erections in males.
* Poor circulation, including Raynaud's phenomenon.
* Long-term suppression of growth and weight loss in pediatric patients.
* Potential for abuse and dependence.

## Misuse of Methylphenidate

We will finish with the examination of the growing utilization of methylphenidate within insights from sales reports.

The sales data collected from various reports provide compelling evidence of the increasing demand for medications containing methylphenidate. Initially launched in 1996, the number of units sold soared from 26,000 in its debut year to an impressive 220,000 by 2005. This trend of substantial growth continued, with sales figures exceeding 600,000 units in 2014.

Findings from the National Medicines Safety Agency (ANSM) highlight the magnitude of this surge. From 1996 to 2012, sales of methylphenidate experienced a significant upswing, witnessing a remarkable leap from 26,000 units to 494,000 units sold during this period. These statistics underscore the escalating popularity and widespread use of methylphenidate over the years.

To ensure the optimal and responsible use of methylphenidate, it is crucial to emphasize the importance of regular reassessment of prescriptions and incorporating withdrawal periods. It is recommended that medical professionals reassess the prescription every 12 months, allowing for periodic evaluation of the patient's progress and adjusting the treatment plan accordingly. By implementing such measures, healthcare providers can ensure the ongoing appropriateness and efficacy of the medication.

Assessing the clinical signs in children, particularly when it comes to evaluating the effects of methylphenidate, presents its own set of challenges. The subjective nature of psychological measures and the individual variability in treatment response contribute to the complexity of this task. It is essential for healthcare professionals to carefully monitor and interpret clinical signs to make informed decisions about the dosage and duration of methylphenidate treatment.

A noteworthy observation in the realm of methylphenidate utilization is the substantial increase in prescriptions between 2003 and 2008, amounting to a staggering 70% rise. This surge prompts us to question whether there might be a deficiency in doctor training regarding the appropriate use of this medication. Further exploration is necessary to investigate the potential impact of improved training on prescribing practices and patient outcomes.

The data derived from sales reports provide valuable insights into the growing utilization of medications containing methylphenidate. By closely examining sales trends, considering psychological measures, and recognizing the challenges in clinical assessment, we gain a comprehensive understanding of this prominent medication. Addressing the potential deficiency in doctor training may further contribute to the responsible use of methylphenidate, ultimately benefiting patients and ensuring optimal healthcare outcomes.
