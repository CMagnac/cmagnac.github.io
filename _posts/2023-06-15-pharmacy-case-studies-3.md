Welcome to our Pharmacy Case Studies series, where we delve into real-life scenarios to provide valuable insights into various health conditions.

In this edition, we focus on breast cancer prevention and treatment, specifically examining the case of a postmenopausal woman. By exploring this case study, we aim to raise awareness about breast cancer risk factors, discuss relevant treatment options, and emphasize the importance of preventive measures.

A postmenopausal 60-year-old woman came to the pharmacy with a treatment prescribed by a medical oncologist.

Ten years ago, she noticed a lump in her breast and under her arm, along with a change in the nipple or areola. She was diagnosed with advanced breast cancer. She has been taking an aromatase inhibitor for the past five years. Two years ago, she was diagnosed with severe osteoporosis, which is considered a "normal" reaction to the aromatase inhibitor drug.

Before delving into further explanations about the treatment, let's take a look at the risk factors of breast cancer.

## Risk factors

The age and lifestyle play important roles in the development of breast cancer.

* Nearly 80% of breast cancers occur after the age of 50.
* Alcohol and tobacco consumption, being overweight, or having little to no physical activity can increase the risk of breast cancer.

Personal and family history also influence the risk of developing breast cancer.

* Women who have had breast, ovarian, or endometrial cancer, or certain breast conditions, have a higher risk compared to other women of the same age.
* Having a family history of breast or ovarian cancer can increase the risk.
* Genetic predisposition to certain cancers, including breast cancer, can be passed down from one generation to another.
* A personal history of exposure to medical radiation before the age of 30, such as high-dose chest radiation therapy for Hodgkin's lymphoma, also increases the risk.
The age and the lifestyle played an important role.

## A Pharmacy Case

Here is the prescription:

* Prolia&reg;60mg (Denosumab) 120mg, 1 subcutaneous injection every 28 days.
* Femara&reg;2.5mg (Letrozole) 1 tablet per day
* Kisqali&reg;200mg (Ribociclib) 3 tablets per day at once during 21 days and 8 days break
* Cacit D3&reg; 500mg (Calcium Carbonate)/440IU (Cholecalciferol) 2 tablets per day, one in the morning and one in the evening.

(IU stands for International Unit)

Treatment for 6 months.

### 3. Kisqali

<blockquote>
Ribociclib is a kinase inhibitor used to treat HR+, HER2- advanced or metastatic breast cancer.

Kisqali (ribociclib) is a selective cyclin-dependent kinase inhibitor, a class of drugs that help slow the progression of cancer by inhibiting two proteins called cyclin-dependent kinase 4 and 6 (CDK4/6). These proteins, when over-activated, can enable cancer cells to grow and divide too quickly. Targeting CDK4/6 with enhanced precision may play a role in ensuring that cancer cells do not continue to replicate uncontrollably.
</blockquote>

## Prevention

Breast cancer is the most common and deadliest cancer in women, surpassing colorectal and lung cancers. Each year, there are nearly 59,000 new cases diagnosed and over 12,100 deaths attributed to breast cancer.

It is recommended that women have their breasts examined (through observation and palpation) once a year, starting from the age of 25, by their general practitioner, gynecologist, or midwife.

Certain factors have been identified as protective against breast cancer, including:

* Late onset of puberty.
* Having a pregnancy before the age of 25.
* Breastfeeding for a duration of 6 months across all pregnancies.
* Early onset of menopause.

Breast cancer remains a significant health concern, affecting thousands of women worldwide. Early detection and timely intervention are crucial for improving survival rates and outcomes. By understanding the risk factors associated with breast cancer and adopting preventive strategies, individuals can take proactive steps to safeguard their health.
