**Neurofeedback** (NFB) is a non-invasive therapeutic method that consists of measuring the cerebral activities of one or more brain areas in a subject and showing them in real-time the recording of this activity. Therefore, patients can learn to control their neuronal activity. This learning can or cannot lead to behavioral changes.

Even though there are still many misunderstandings about how our brain works, the twentieth century has been rich in scientific discoveries and comprehension about it. Hans Berger discovered **Electroencephalography** (EEG) in 1924. In 1972, Sterman created the first protocol of NFB. The first experimentation of NFB for **Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder** (ADHD) patients was led by Lubar and Shouse in 1976.

Neurofeedback (NFB) is an alternative treatment for neurodevelopmental disorders such as ADHD. ADHD is a disturbance of dopamine transmission or the dopaminergic system. It is a chronic disease that impacts the social and scholarly life of the youngest. The symptoms are characterized by a triad: hyperactivity, impulsivity, and attention deficit.

The diagnosis is clinical, and many consultations are necessary to confirm it. There is a specific drug treatment for ADHD: the **methylphenidate** (MPH) of the amphetamine classes. The effects of long-term pharmacological treatment are uncertain, and many adverse effects can appear.

That is why it is necessary to explore another therapeutic path such as NFB.

<br>

## Types of NFB

Two types of NFB exist in relation to the tools.

Firstly, the **NFB guided by EEG** (EEG-NFB). 25 to 50 sessions of almost one hour are necessary to get some results.

This method applies to three **psychiatric disturbances**: ADHD, depressive episodes, and anxiety and addiction disorder.

Secondly, the **NFB guided by Magnetic Resonance Imaging** (MRI-NFB).

This method applies to four psychiatric disturbances: depressive episodes, addiction disorder, personality disorder, and obsessive-compulsive disorder.

In the case of EEG-NFB, doctors analyze the cerebral waves that are different in our state of mind.

<br>

## The cerebral brainwaves

There are distinct cerebral rhythms that can be classified with Greek letters.

| Brainwaves      | Frequency |
| :----------:    | :-------: |
| Delta (&delta;) |  < 4Hz    |
| Thêta (&theta;) |  4-7 Hz   |
| Alpha (&alpha;) | 8-12 Hz   |
| Beta  (&beta;)  | 13-25 Hz  |
| Beta-1          | 13-16 Hz  |
| Beta-2          | 17-25 Hz  |
| Gamma (&gamma;) | 25-50 Hz  |

The connection between brainwaves and our states of being are as follows:

* Delta brainwaves correspond to **deep sleep and coma**.
* Theta brainwaves correspond to **memory and emotions**.
* Alpha brainwaves correspond to **relaxation, meditation, eyes closed**.
* Beta brainwaves correspond to **eyes open**.
* Gamma brainwaves correspond to **consciousness**.

Neurological diseases are associated with a **default or an excess** of these cortical rhythms.

The doctor evaluates the brain activity with an EEG and a **Brain Computer Interface** (BCI) is used to show the visual or auditory feedback to the patient. Consequently, the patient handles or regulates their own brain activity.

<br>

## Principle of NFB

The neurofeedback principle is associated with a psychophysiological loop.

There are six steps to understand how NFB is working:

1. **Cerebral activity recording** : recording the cerebral activity with an EEG gear.
2. **Signal boosting** : amplification of the signal with an electronic box called the amplifier, which is connected both to the EEG and the software. The signal is interpreted by the NFB algorithms.
3. **Informatic scan** : scanning the EEG signal into a binary signal to be understood by the software.
4. **Real-time signal processing** : the algorithms process the binary signal associated with the NFB protocol.
5. **Visual or auditory feedback** : a feedback returns to the patient depending on their cerebral activities.
6. **Learning cognitive strategy** : as a consequence, the patient searches for new cognitive strategies.
