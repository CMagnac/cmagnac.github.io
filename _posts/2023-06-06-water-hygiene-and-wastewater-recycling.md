Water is an essential resource that sustains life on Earth. However, it can also serve as a vector for the transmission of various gastro-intestinal infections. To safeguard public health, stringent regulatory requirements exist to ensure water hygiene and the proper treatment of wastewater.

In this article, we will explore the significance of water hygiene, the common bacterial agents transmitted through water, water quality standards, European regulations, and the pressing issue of water scarcity.

## Waterborne Bacterial Agents

Several bacteria, fungi, viruses, and protozoa can be transmitted through water, leading to gastro-intestinal infections. Among the bacterial agents commonly found in water sources are Vibrio cholerae, Typhoid salmonella, Enteric Salmonella, Shigella spp, Campylobacter jejuni, Enteropathogenic Escherichia coli, Yersinia enterocolitica, Legionella pneumophila, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Chlamydia trachomatis, and Dermatophytes.

Additionally, various viruses such as Adenovirus, Enterovirus, Hepatitis A and E viruses, Norovirus, and Rotavirus, as well as protozoa like Giardia intestinalis, Cryptosporidium parvum, Acanthamoebae spp., and Naegleria fowleri, can pose significant health risks when present in water.

## Water Quality and Treatment Methods

To ensure water quality, effective waste elimination and disinfection methods are employed. Water treatment processes typically include mechanical methods, biological clarification, chemical sanitation, secondary clarification, flocculation or ultrafiltration, and treatment with methanogenic bacteria. These processes aid in removing dirt, microorganisms, and harmful agents from water sources.

Meeting the microbiological requirements for water intended for human consumption is of utmost importance, with strict regulations in place. For instance, Escherichia coli and Enterococci should be absent in 100ml of water intended for human consumption, as mandated by European Directive 98/83/EC and the Public Health Code.

## European Regulations and Wastewater Treatment

Europe places significant emphasis on wastewater treatment to minimize the impact on the environment and protect public health. For example, in the Strasbourg Eurometropolis, 98.5% of wastewater is treated at the Strasbourg-La Wantzenau wastewater treatment plant.

The treatment process involves pre-treatment stages such as desanding and oil removal, primary treatment, biological treatment, and energy recovery. Biogas produced during sludge digestion is transformed into electrical and thermal energy, ensuring a sustainable and environmentally friendly approach to wastewater management.

## The Challenge of Water Scarcity

While efforts are being made to ensure water hygiene and wastewater treatment, it is crucial to acknowledge the global issue of water scarcity. Approximately two billion people worldwide still lack access to clean drinking water.

Addressing this challenge requires sustainable solutions, including water conservation, efficient use of water resources, and the development of innovative technologies for water purification and desalination. Combating climate change, which exacerbates water scarcity, is also vital for long-term water security.

## Conclusion

Water hygiene and wastewater recycling are vital components of public health and environmental protection. Adhering to regulatory requirements, implementing effective treatment methods, and promoting responsible water consumption are essential steps towards ensuring safe water for all.

Additionally, global collaboration is necessary to address the pressing issue of water scarcity, aiming to provide clean drinking water to the millions of people still in need.

By prioritizing water hygiene and embracing sustainable practices, we can safeguard public health, preserve ecosystems, and create a healthier and more equitable future for all.
